Bruno Loureiro argues that many football errors look obvious on the surface but stem from different underlying causes. His framework offers coaches a clearer way to separate execution, choice, spacing and body-orientation problems when reviewing the same broken sequence.

<!---more--->

Bruno Loureiro's [Avaliação do Erro no Futebol](https://universidadedofutebol.com.br/2026/04/09/avaliacao-do-erro-no-futebol/), published by Universidade do Futebol, is best understood as a coaching-analysis piece rather than a data study. Its aim is practical: make post-match review and training feedback more precise by distinguishing different kinds of error that can produce the same visible outcome.

Loureiro's starting point is broader than the usual idea of error. In his account, error is not limited to the obvious miss — a shot wide, a loose touch, a pass straight out of play. It is any deviation from what the situation and the game model required. That is why a pass can still be wrong even if it reaches its teammate, or why a player can occupy the nominally correct zone and still undermine the move through their body shape.

That framing gives the article its coaching value. In Loureiro's build-up example — a centre-back trying to find a midfielder and failing to progress the ball into the next third — the visible failure is easy to spot. The harder and more useful question is what kind of mistake actually produced it.

He proposes four main categories.

**Technical (Execution)**

This is the most obvious category: the pass does not arrive, arrives with the wrong weight, arrives too high, reaches the teammate on the wrong foot, or the receiving touch prevents the next action. One of Loureiro's clearest points is that an action can look superficially successful and still be technically wrong. A completed pass is not necessarily a good pass if it worsens the receiver's next move.

**Decision-Making (Choice)**

Here the action may be technically clean but conceptually wrong. Loureiro leans on the FIGC's framing and the OODA sequence — observe, orient, decide, act — to argue that execution is only the final stage. In his build-up example, this category includes misreading numerical conditions, missing a positional or qualitative advantage, misunderstanding the relevant tactical principle, or lacking enough experience of that exact situation. The article points toward representative repetition as part of the answer, though it does so as coaching logic rather than as a tested intervention.

**Space Occupation**

This category shifts the focus from the single action to the relations between players. Loureiro's point is that interaction depends on spacing being right for the team's idea. If teammates stand too close together, the press can lock onto multiple players at once. If they stand too far apart, the ball carrier loses connections. If the structure blocks the goalkeeper from being used as an extra player, a possible numerical edge disappears. Importantly, the article does not suggest one universal spacing rule; the correct occupation depends on the game model.

**Individual Tactics**

This covers details such as body orientation, positioning and movement relative to pressure. In the offensive examples, Loureiro gives special importance to the receiver's posture: a closed body shape, receiving with the back to play, or standing with heels flat on the ground. These are treated as tactical-preparatory faults rather than purely technical ones. That distinction matters because it changes the kind of feedback a coach would give and the kind of training task they might design.

Loureiro also adds an important caveat outside the four-part taxonomy: the opponent matters. Not every lost ball should be read purely as your own technical or tactical failure. Sometimes the decisive factor is an opponent action that changed the time-space available. That is a useful guardrail in a sport where video review can become too eager to assign blame to the team in possession.

A second caveat appears later in the article under what Loureiro calls the hidden variable. He notes that fatigue, game reading and emotional state influence actions, but argues that analysis should still define error through the spatial, temporal and relational indicators visible in the play itself. In other words, he is not denying those hidden influences; he is trying to keep the first layer of coaching analysis anchored to observable football behaviour.

The main strength of the piece is therefore its language. It gives coaches and analysts a shared way to say that a failed sequence was not simply "bad execution". A pass to the wrong foot, a poor body orientation, a bad spacing relation and a mistaken read of pressure can all end the same move, but they do not point to the same correction.

The article is also clear, if mostly implicitly, about its limits. It does not tell us how often each category appears, whether coaches would classify the same clip in the same way, or how reliably the framework could be turned into a larger measurement system. Its value is more immediate and practical: it sharpens diagnosis inside coaching review.

That is what makes the framework interesting. It does not reduce football error to one final mistake. Instead, it encourages coaches to work backward from the breakdown and identify which layer of the action sequence actually failed. For analysts and coaching staffs trying to make feedback more precise, that is already a useful contribution.
